# Story Arc Patterns for Video Presentations

This reference defines narrative structures for building compelling, persuasive video
presentations. The goal is to turn any topic into a story that moves the audience from
understanding to conviction.

These patterns work for any subject — tech demos, event promos, product launches, concert
recaps, educational content, and more.

## The Core Story Arc: Problem -> Tension -> Resolution

Every great presentation follows this fundamental shape:

### Act 1: The World As It Is (Slides 1-3)
**Purpose**: Orient the audience. Establish shared reality.
- **Title slide**: Bold claim or provocative question. Set the emotional tone.
- **Context slide**: What's happening in the industry/community/world right now?
- **The Pain**: What specific problem or friction does the audience feel today? Be concrete. Use numbers, quotes, or scenarios they recognize.

### Act 2: The Turning Point (Slides 4-6)
**Purpose**: Create tension. Show why the status quo is unsustainable.
- **Why now?**: What has changed that makes this moment critical? New technology? New threat? New opportunity?
- **The gap**: Show the distance between where we are and where we need to be. Use contrast — before/after, with/without, old way/new way.
- **The insight**: Introduce the key idea, approach, or solution that changes everything. This is the "aha" moment.

### Act 3: The New World (Slides 7-9)
**Purpose**: Paint the future. Make it feel achievable.
- **How it works**: Demonstrate the solution in action. Architecture diagrams, workflows, demos, examples.
- **Proof**: Evidence that this works. Case studies, benchmarks, testimonials, live demos.
- **What you get**: Concrete benefits. Speed, cost, quality, experience. Quantify where possible.

### Act 4: The Call to Action (Slide 10)
**Purpose**: Mobilize. Tell them exactly what to do next.
- Clear next steps
- Resources and links
- Who to contact or where to go
- Timeline if applicable

## Variation: The "Myth-Busting" Arc

Good for challenging conventional wisdom or introducing paradigm shifts.

1. Title: The provocative thesis
2. The common belief (what "everyone knows")
3. Evidence that the belief is wrong or incomplete
4. The real story (with evidence)
5. What this means for you
6. The new approach
7. How it works
8. Results / proof
9. Getting started
10. Resources and CTA

## Variation: The "Journey" Arc

Good for case studies, project retrospectives, event recaps, or transformation stories.

1. Title: Where we ended up (start with the destination)
2. Where we started (the messy beginning)
3. First attempt (what we tried, what failed)
4. The breakthrough (what we learned)
5. Building momentum (iteration, improvement)
6. The architecture / approach (how it works now)
7. Results (metrics, outcomes)
8. Lessons learned
9. What's next
10. How you can do this too

## Variation: The "Showcase" Arc

Good for concert promos, event highlights, portfolio pieces, and creative showcases.

1. Title: Bold statement or event name with date/location
2. The vibe: Set the mood — what makes this special?
3. The lineup / highlights: Who or what is featured?
4. Behind the scenes: How it comes together
5. The experience: What attendees/viewers can expect
6. Social proof: Past events, testimonials, press
7. Key details: Dates, venues, tickets, pricing
8. CTA: Get tickets / register / watch / follow

## Variation: The "How-To" Arc

Good for tutorials, educational content, and step-by-step guides.

1. Title: What you'll learn
2. Why it matters: The value of this knowledge
3. Prerequisites: What you need before starting
4. Step 1: Foundation
5. Step 2: Core technique
6. Step 3: Advanced application
7. Common mistakes to avoid
8. Final result: What success looks like
9. Practice exercise or challenge
10. Resources and next steps

## Slide Design Principles for Storytelling

### One idea per slide
Every slide should be reducible to a single sentence. If you need two ideas, make two slides.

### Headlines tell the story
A viewer who only reads slide headlines should understand the full narrative arc. Headlines
are assertions, not labels.

**Bad**: "Architecture Overview"
**Good**: "A Fully Local Stack Eliminates Cloud Dependencies"

**Bad**: "Benefits"
**Good**: "Teams Ship 3x Faster With Air-Gapped AI Assist"

**Bad**: "Event Details"
**Good**: "Three Nights of Non-Stop Music Under the Stars"

### Progressive disclosure
Don't dump everything at once. Each slide should reveal the next piece of the puzzle,
building anticipation.

### Emotional rhythm
Alternate between tension and relief. After a slide about a hard problem, show a slide
about the elegant solution. After dense technical content, give a breathing slide with
a big quote or visual.

### The Rule of Three
Group supporting points in threes. Three benefits. Three acts. Three proof points. The
human brain finds triads satisfying and memorable.

## Content-Specific Tips

### Technical / Product Presentations
- Show only the most relevant 5-10 lines of code
- Use monospace font with syntax-appropriate coloring
- Add a brief annotation explaining what's happening
- Architecture diagrams: 5-7 components maximum
- Show data flow with arrows
- Highlight the "new" or "different" part with the accent color

### Event / Concert Promos
- Lead with energy — bold visuals, dynamic text
- Feature names and dates prominently
- Use the accent color to match event branding
- Keep text minimal — let the mood speak
- Include social handles and ticket links

### Comparison / Before-After
- Side-by-side layout with clear visual distinction
- Make the winner obvious through design emphasis
- Use color-coding: muted for "before", accent for "after"

### Data / Metrics
- One key number per slide, displayed LARGE
- Context below in smaller text
- Source attribution at bottom

## Sourcing and Attribution
- Include source URLs at the bottom of slides that cite external data
- Use "Source: [name], [year]" format
- When web search is available, actively research current statistics and quotes to strengthen the narrative
